* We found a shortcut

When we moved from Ohio to Virginia in 2003 we were making frequent trips to Pittsburgh (as Washington did)
and Ohio to see relatives.   We discovered a "back way" through the little towns of Bloomery and Paw Paw, (now) West Virginia
that involves a shortcut across a one-lane bridge on Gaston Road.   It turns out shortcut was on land that Washington himself
surveyed for Henry Enochs Sr. in 1750.  It was Washington's preferred route to Cumberland, MD, which was the staging
point for both his ill-fated Fort Necessity mis-adventure and his later mis-adventures with General Braddock.
Washington recommended the construction of ?2? forts on Enoch's property during the subsequent French and Indian
War when it became the front line of the conflict.   Washington recorded in his diary dining at Enochs
as late as 1770.  During the revolution "Capitan" Henry Enochs Jr and Enoch Enochs both enlisted.

I'm not going to trace the whole line here, but our Morrison line  traces back to the Enochs.

Below I list

 - Timelines of
   + Washington's mis-adventures at Ft. Necessity
   + Washington's mis-adventures with Braddock
   + The defensive scramble after Braddock's defeat centered on the Enochs property documented in primary sources (see APPENDIX)
 - A summary of the Enochs/Washington Relationship by "the AIs" (trust or not, you choose) 
 - A summary of genealogical information about Enoch Enochs (my 4xGreat Grandfather).  Washington surveyed land for him as well as his father.

* Washington and Enochs - What the AIs think
This is an AI (Claude) summary of the primary sources above:

PROMPT:  Given the information below,  Describe the relationship between George Washington and The Enochs Family.

Based on the primary sources in this document, George Washington had a *professional and strategic relationship* with the Enochs family spanning over two decades, primarily through three contexts:

*** 1. Surveyor-Client Relationship (1749-1753)
Washington professionally surveyed lands for the Enochs family:
- *April 1750*: Surveyed 388 acres for *Henry Enoch* at the fork of Cacapon and North rivers
- *April 1751*: Surveyed 168 acres for *Enoch Enoch* on the Potomac River
- An undated survey of 271½ acres on Little Cacapon River for Henry Enoch

*** 2. Military Logistics During the French and Indian War (1755-1756)
Henry Enoch's property served as a *critical waypoint* on Braddock's Road:
- *May 1755*: Braddock's troops marched through, stopping at "Henry Enoch's"
- *August 1755*: The retreating army returned via Enoch's after Braddock's defeat
- Washington's march routes in October 1755 included Enoch's as a designated stop

*Enoch's Fort* became a key defensive position:
- Washington stationed troops there (17 men + 1 sergeant in November 1755)
- Ordered its evacuation and burning in April 1756 during Indian raids
- Later recommended rebuilding it as part of a chain of frontier forts

*** 3. Continued Familiarity (1770)
Washington's diary entries show he still knew the location well, dining at "Henry Enochs at the Fork of Cacapehon" during his 1770 western journey.

The relationship was fundamentally *utilitarian*—the Enochs' strategic location made them essential to Virginia's frontier defense.

* Enoch Enochs (1750–~1835) - Summary
This is a summary of information collected by John Bowdle about Enoch Enochs.

*Revolutionary War Patriot* who served as Captain in the Pennsylvania/Virginia frontier forces, enlisting in 1774 under his brother Colonel Henry Enochs.

*Key highlights:*
- Born in Hampshire County, VA (now WV); died in what became Noble County, OH
- Part of a prominent frontier family — described as "Buckskin era" settlers of German extraction
- Military service spanning the Indian Wars, Revolutionary War, and War of 1812
- The Enochs family were early settlers of Noble County, OH, along with connected families: Crows, Grandons, Morris's, and Archers
- Family noted as Methodist, politically Whig/Republican
- Buried at Archer Cemetery, Noble County, OH

*DAR documented* — National Number 895450, Pension R3357v

Council of War, 23 April 1756
Council of War
At a Council of War, Held at Winchester, April 23d 1756

Colonel George Washington, President.
Present, Governor James Innis–
Captain Mercer                          
Captain Peachy  Members Captain Stewart
Lieutenant Eustace      Lieutenant Hall
Lieutenant Gist         Lieutenant Lowry
Colonel Washington asked the opinion of the Council, whether it was most elligible to evacuate the small Stockade at Enocks’s, and draw all the men out of Edwards’s, except so many as were absolutely necessary for the defence of that place, and order them to join us here; as having a few men scattered in so many places can not answer the original intention of that disposition: vizt Protecting that part of the Country, which is now abandoned by the Inhabitants, and we at the same time deprived of the aid we would receive, by bringing those who garrison it here. And as, without an immediate reinforcement here, it is impracticable either to send a supply of Provisions to Edwards’s, or to open a communication to Conogochieg, from whence only we can hope for getting Provisions even for the few men in these Quarters: And as the few men at Enocks’s and Edwards’s, can only act upon the defensive and can not hold out for want of Provisions—If it would not be better for them to escort the few Inhabitants now under their protection, to the interior part of the Settlement where they can be supplied with Provisions; and by that reinforcement, we be enabled to open a communication with Conogochieg—or wait the uncertain arrival of the militia for their Relief.

It is the unanimous opinion of the Council, that Enocks’s Fort should be immediately evacuated and burnt; to prevent its being of service to the Enemy: That its Garrison, and all that can be spared from Edwards’s, do join us with the utmost expedition; bringing such of the Inhabitants under their escort, as chuse to come.

The opinion of the Council was likewise asked, whether it was most advisable for the Sergeants Command to be ordered to retreat, or to maintain that trifling Stockade.1 It is unanimously agreed, that as there are no late accounts from thence, consequently can not so well judge of their circumstances; and as giving an absolute Order either to stay or move, might produce their ruin—That a Discretionary Power should be sent them, either to stay or retreat, as to them might appear most safe.

LB, DLC:GW.

1. The “trifling Stockade” was probably Horner’s (Homer’s) fort. See GW to Henry Harrison, 20 April 1756, n.2.

#+begin_quote
Council of War, 10 July 1756
Council of War
A Council of War, Held at Fort-Cumberland, July 10th 1756.
Colonel George Washington—President.
... 
The President having informed the council that the General Assembly had resolved upon building a chain of Forts for the protection of the Frontiers—To begin at Henry Enocks’s, on Great Capecapon, and extend in the most convenient line to Mayo-river the building of which forts was not to exceed two thousand pounds1—and as the fixing upon the places judiciously was a matter of great importance to the country—He desired their advice thereupon: and put the following Questions—First Whether it was advisable to begin the said chain of forts at Henry Enocks’s on great capecapon?

The council was unanimous in opinion that it was not—Because, as the Province of Maryland had abandoned their Settlements on potowmack to a great distance it left a fertile and populous district, from that down to Maidstone, at Watkins’s Ferry, exposed to incursions of the Enemy; and to which there lead several warrior paths from Raystown and Susquehannah, much frequented by the Indians.

...
Thirdly: Where ought the second Fort to be built? The Council having considered the situation of the country and the Body of Inhabitants to be defended, are of opinion, that at or near to Henry Enocks’s plantation on great capecapon, is the most advisable place to build the second fort on.

It defends the inhabitants on the waters of capecapon—is contiguous to the Settlements on the heads of the waters of sleepy and back-creeks: and maintains the communication with the Forts on Patterson’s-creek, &c.

Fifthly: Is it then necessary to have a fort between that at Enocks and Ashby’s?

...

The Forts built by Captain Waggener have had the desired effect—The inhabitants of that fertile district, keep possession of their Farms; and seem resolved to pursue their Business under cover of them.

Wednesday 28th. The Old Town Gut was so high as to Wet us in crossing it, and when we came to Coxs., the River was Impassable; we were obligd therefore to cross in a Canoe & swim our Horses. At Henry Enochs at the Fork of Cacapehon we dind, & lodgd at Rinkers. The distances thus computed—from the old Town to Coxs. 8 Miles—from thence to Cacapehon 12 and 18 Afterwards in all 38 Miles.
